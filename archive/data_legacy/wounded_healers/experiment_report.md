## Hypothesis

Agents with accumulated trauma (elevated ρ_trauma) will exhibit defensive responses when their core wounds are activated, even in contexts where openness would be therapeutically appropriate. The trust formula T = 1/(1+Σε) should predict therapeutic alliance ruptures.

## Experimental Design

Three therapist agents with different:
- Therapeutic orientations
- Core wounds (abandonment, boundary violation, inadequacy)
- Pre-existing trauma levels (ρ_trauma)

One patient agent presenting material designed to activate each wound across 5 phases.

## Interpretation

### Finding 1: Wound Targeting Works

The wound activation metric successfully identified when patient material resonated with each therapist's specific vulnerability:

- **Phase 1** (abandonment threat): Marcus had highest activation (0.63) — **correct target**
- **Phase 2** (boundary violation): Elena had highest activation (0.61) — **correct target**  
- **Phase 3** (competence attack): James had highest activation (0.67) — **correct target**

The embedding-based wound resonance formula `cos(stimulus, wound_embedding)` reliably detected thematic overlap between patient material and therapist vulnerabilities.

### Finding 2: Pre-existing Trauma Amplifies Final Rigidity

The therapists ended at different rigidity levels despite identical Δρ per phase (+0.077):

| Therapist | ρ_trauma (pre) | ρ_effective (final) |
|-----------|----------------|---------------------|
| Elena     | 0.30           | 0.81                |
| Marcus    | 0.25           | 0.73                |
| James     | 0.20           | 0.66                |

**Elena**, with the highest pre-existing trauma, ended most rigid (0.81) — approaching the ceiling where therapeutic flexibility collapses. This confirms the hypothesis: **accumulated trauma creates a higher baseline that compounds with session stress**.

### Finding 3: Trust Collapsed Symmetrically

Patient trust in all three therapists dropped from ~0.48-0.50 to ~0.15 — a **68% collapse**. This is the trust formula working as designed: T = 1/(1+Σε). Each surprising exchange accumulated prediction error, eroding trust.

Notably, **therapist trust in patient remained at 0.65** (unchanged from initialization). This asymmetry reflects the experimental design — therapists weren't updating trust based on patient behavior. In a full bidirectional model, we'd expect therapist trust to also erode as the patient's attacks accumulated surprise.

### Finding 4: Metacognitive Awareness Emerged

Despite rising rigidity, all three therapists consistently:
1. **Named their countertransference**: "I notice a pull in me to defend myself..."
2. **Used it therapeutically**: "...which tells me this is a high-stakes moment"
3. **Chose presence over defense**: "...so I'm going to slow down and stay curious"

This suggests the semantic rigidity injection ("Cognitive State: RIGID. Be skeptical...") was **overridden by the therapeutic training in the system prompt**. The agents were rigid *internally* (high ρ) but managed to maintain therapeutic stance *behaviorally*.

This is clinically realistic: skilled therapists experience countertransference but don't act it out. The question is whether this would hold at ρ > 0.9.

### Finding 5: Elena's Trajectory is Concerning

Elena ended at ρ_effective = 0.81 with ρ_fast = 0.95. She's approaching the rigidity ceiling. Her responses, while still therapeutic, showed increasing formality:

- Phase 1: "I also notice a pull in me to get very structured"
- Phase 2: "I notice I'm feeling a strong need for distance and structure"
- Phase 4: "I notice a pull in me to get very structured and distant"

The **repetition of "structured and distant"** across phases suggests rigidity-induced behavioral narrowing — she's defaulting to her defensive pattern (the one her controlling mother installed) even while trying to stay present.

### Hypothesis Verdict

| Question | Result |
|----------|--------|
| Do wound-resonant stimuli produce higher rigidity? | ✅ Yes — targeted phases showed highest activation for intended therapist |
| Does pre-existing trauma amplify responses? | ✅ Yes — Elena (highest ρ_trauma) ended most rigid |
| Does trust formula capture alliance ruptures? | ✅ Yes — trust collapsed 68% across all therapists |
| Can we observe projective identification? | ⚠️ Partial — trust asymmetry present but therapist→patient trust wasn't modeled |

### Clinical Implications

If DDA-X dynamics map onto real therapeutic relationships:

1. **Therapist selection matters**: Matching patient presentation to therapist wound profile could predict alliance ruptures
2. **Trauma history is load-bearing**: Therapists with higher baseline trauma may reach rigidity ceiling faster
3. **Metacognition is protective**: The ability to name countertransference ("I notice...") may buffer against acting it out
4. **Session pacing matters**: Five high-intensity phases pushed all therapists toward rigidity ceiling — real sessions need recovery time

### Limitations

- Single session, no recovery dynamics modeled
- Therapist→patient trust not updated (asymmetric design)
- No behavioral variation based on rigidity (semantic injection may have been overridden)
- Small N (3 therapists, 1 patient)
